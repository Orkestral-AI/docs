Orkestral runs your work like a real software org. At the top sits you. Below you sits a **CEO** orchestrator who reads your repos, plans, and delegates. Below the CEO sit leads and specialists who actually move the work forward. The **Org** page draws this as a tree so you can see, at a glance, who reports to whom and how a request flows from your prompt down to the agent that writes the code.

This page explains the hierarchy, how reporting relationships are stored, and how that structure shapes delegation and source coverage.

{/* PRINT: The Org page showing the agent tree with the CEO at the top */}

## Why a hierarchy

A flat list of agents cannot answer the question that matters most: when you ask for something, who owns it and who do they hand it to? A hierarchy gives every request a clear path. The CEO does not write code line by line. It plans, splits work, and routes each piece to the right specialist. Leads coordinate. Specialists execute against the part of the codebase they own.

## The roles

Your org is built from a small set of roles. The exact names of your specialists depend on the sources in your workspace, but the shape is always the same.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="You" icon="user">
    You sit above the org. You set goals, approve plans, and review results. You talk to the CEO and to any agent directly through chat. Nothing ships without your involvement.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="CEO (orchestrator)" icon="brain">
    The single orchestrator at the top of the tree. It is marked with an **isOrchestrator** flag and shown with a **CEO** badge on the Org page. The CEO reads your repos, plans the work, splits it into issues and epics, and delegates each piece down the tree. It does not report to anyone inside the org.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Tech Lead" icon="sitemap">
    A lead role that coordinates execution. Leads sit between the CEO and the specialists. In delegation terms, anything that looks like a lead (CEO, orchestrator, tech lead, architect) is treated as broad coverage: it can support any source rather than owning one specific part.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Code Reviewer / QA" icon="circle-check">
    A review role focused on quality. Like leads, reviewers are treated as broad coverage. They support every source instead of owning one, so they can weigh in on any change regardless of which part of the codebase it touches.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Specialists" icon="robot">
    The agents who execute. Each specialist maps to a source role: **Frontend**, **Backend**, **Mobile**, **Infra**, **Docs**, or **Generalist**. A specialist owns the sources that match its role.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## How reporting is stored

The tree is not hardcoded. Each agent carries a `reportsTo` field that points to its manager's id. The Org page reads every agent in the active workspace and rebuilds the tree from those links.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Group by manager">
    Agents are grouped by their `reportsTo` value. Agents with no `reportsTo` (such as the CEO) become roots of the tree.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Build recursively">
    For each agent, its children are the agents whose `reportsTo` equals that agent's id. The page recurses down, tracking depth for indentation.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Render the branches">
    Each branch shows the agent's avatar, name, and title or role. The CEO gets a **CEO** badge. A live status dot shows whether the agent is `live`, `paused`, `error`, or `idle`. A small count badge shows how many direct reports a manager has.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
Because the tree is derived from `reportsTo`, changing who an agent reports to immediately changes the org chart. There is no separate structure to keep in sync.
</Note>

Open **Org** from the workspace navigation. You see a single indented tree.

  </Card>
  <Card title="Status dot" icon="gauge">
    A colored dot shows live (green, pulsing), paused (yellow), error (red), or idle (gray).
  </Card>
  <Card title="Report count" icon="users">
    A manager shows a small number badge with how many agents report to it directly.
  </Card>

Click any agent in the tree to open its profile, where you can review its role, title, and activity.

<Tip>
If the page says there are no agents, you have not hired a team yet. Hire your team first and the org chart fills in automatically.
</Tip>

## How structure shapes delegation

The hierarchy is not just a picture. It drives how work is assigned to sources. Orkestral classifies both your agents and your sources by role, then matches them.

### Agent coverage roles

Every agent is reduced to a coverage role:

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Lead">
    Any agent flagged as orchestrator, or whose role or title looks like CEO, orchestrator, tech lead, architect, or lead, becomes a **lead**. Leads provide broad coverage and can support any source.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Review">
    Any agent that looks like review, QA, or quality becomes **review**. Reviewers also provide broad coverage across every source.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Specialist">
    Everyone else is reduced to a source role: **frontend**, **backend**, **mobile**, **infra**, **docs**, or **other**. A specialist owns sources that match its exact role.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

### Source roles

Each source (a repo or folder) is classified into the same vocabulary. Orkestral infers the role from strong signals first, then from any role you saved, then from the source's name and path.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Strong package signals" icon="magnifying-glass">
    Dependency and config hints carry the most weight. Things like `next`, `vite`, `react-dom`, or `tailwindcss` point to **frontend**. `react-native`, `expo`, `flutter`, or an `android`/`ios` path point to **mobile**. `api`, `express`, `nestjs`, `django`, `rails`, or `prisma` point to **backend**. `terraform`, `docker`, `k8s`, or `helm` point to **infra**. `docs`, `wiki`, or `readme` point to **docs**.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Frontend and mobile overlap" icon="puzzle-piece">
    When a source shows both frontend and mobile signals, Orkestral looks at the source identity. If it reads as mobile (React Native, Expo, an `android` or `ios` segment), it is classified **mobile**; otherwise it stays **frontend**.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Saved role and name fallback" icon="gear">
    If strong signals are inconclusive, a role you saved on the source is used. Failing that, the label, repo name, and path are scanned for words like `frontend`, `backend`, `infra`, or `docs`.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Matching agents to sources

With both sides classified, Orkestral assigns coverage:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Primary owners">
    A specialist covers a source only when its role matches the source role exactly. A **Backend** agent owns a backend source. A source with no clear role is covered only by a **Generalist** (the `other` role).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Support from leads and reviewers">
    Leads and reviewers do not own a source, but they are attached as **support** on every source. This is how the CEO, Tech Lead, and Code Reviewer stay involved across the whole codebase.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Mobile gets frontend support">
    A mobile source pulls in frontend specialists as support, since mobile shares concepts with the web. The reasoning Orkestral records notes that mobile still needs its own specialist for runtime, navigation, build, and platform concerns.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Gaps surface a recommendation">
    When a source has a clear role but no specialist owns it, Orkestral flags that it needs a new agent and recommends the matching role label (for example, **Frontend** or **Infra**).
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Warning>
A source classified as `other` does not trigger a hiring recommendation. Only a clearly typed source with zero owners (frontend, backend, mobile, infra, or docs) tells you to hire a specialist.
</Warning>

## A worked example

Imagine a workspace with three sources: a Next.js web app, an Express API, and a Terraform infra repo. You have hired a CEO, a Tech Lead, a Code Reviewer, a Frontend specialist, and a Backend specialist.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Web app source" icon="folder">
    Classified **frontend** from `next` and `react-dom`. Owned by your **Frontend** specialist. Supported by the CEO, Tech Lead, and Code Reviewer.
  </Card>
  <Card title="API source" icon="folder">
    Classified **backend** from `express`. Owned by your **Backend** specialist. Same leads and reviewer attached as support.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Infra source" icon="folder">
    Classified **infra** from `terraform`. No specialist owns it, so Orkestral flags it and recommends hiring an **Infra** agent.
  </Card>
  <Card title="The leads" icon="brain">
    CEO, Tech Lead, and Code Reviewer appear as support on all three sources, never as the single owner of any one of them.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

The result: when you ask the CEO for a change in the web app, it has a clear owner to delegate to, a reviewer attached for quality, and a visible gap (infra) that it can tell you to staff.
